This work closely connects to my role on [the board of directors of the German Association for Digital Humanities](https://digitalhumanities.de/der-vorstand/) (_Digital Humanities im deutschsprachigen Raum_, DHd). 

Following an extended focus on data feminism, the current projects of the working group are increasingly turning towards the study of data work within digital humanities  {% cite GengnagelLang2026 %}. 

In this context, my own research has also expanded to include critical AI. This includes articulating critical concerns regarding the use of large language models in digital and computational humanities  {% cite Lang2026LLM_criticalHPSS %}, as well as addressing issues such as carbon reporting and the environmental impact of computational research  {% cite LangEtAl2025EnvironmentalFootprint‎ %}. 

Through this work, I have increasingly developed what I describe as a critical digital humanities or critical computational humanities perspective.

My research has also examined dataset documentation practices  {% cite Lang2025DocumentingDatasets %}. 

At DHd 2023, the working group organised a workshop on data feminism  {% cite ‎dhd-workshop-datafem2023‎ %}, the results of which were subsequently reported in blog posts published in both English  {% cite Borek2023a %} and German  {% cite Borek2023b %}. At the same conference, we co-organised a panel  {% cite Gengnagel2023b %} and also co-organised a panel at the international DH2023 conference in Graz that same year {% cite dh2023borderlands %}. I further represented the working group in a panel on failure in DH {% cite scheitern2023 %} that was reported on here {% cite Lang2024Scheitern %}.

At DHd2026, I represented the working group in a panel about the dark sides of DH {% cite ArnoldEtAl2026_DarkSidesDH %}. 

Our longer-term engagement with data feminism and (the gender) data gap(s)  culminated in the publication of a _ZfdG_ working paper on data feminism in 2026  {% cite LangSuarezCronauer2026_BeyondDataFeminism %}. Complementing this work, we published a more focused study on the gender data gap in the _Computational Humanities Research_ journal: In the article, “Dataset Audits for Mitigating Data Gaps,” we propose the dataset audit as a practical strategy for identifying and addressing data gaps while discussing the gender data gap more broadly  {% cite ‎LangSuarezCronauer2026‎ %}.

My research will focus increasingly on data work and its influence on processes of knowledge production in the future. This research is closely connected to critical AI studies, which increasingly recognise data and data work as central sources of bias and unethical outcomes in computational systems. 

Recently, I have developed my early reflections on computational humanities in the form of a blog post  {% cite lang_computationalToxic_Ninja_2020 %} into a piece investigating invisibilised labour in Computational Humanities contexts through the lenses of data work and the invisible technician discourse {% cite Lang2027_TwoTierCH_Feminisms %}.

In my forthcoming book, I aim to articulate a disciplinary ethics that moves beyond mere compliance, providing a framework for critically engaging with the epistemic and ethical challenges posed by data-driven research.


It is an area that I particularly enjoy, and it will form the basis of my next book project after the completion of my habilitation. The book will investigate biases and distortions in datasets. As scholarship increasingly relies on datasets that are inherently fragmented (especially in historical disciplines, but also in contemporary contexts), the project examines the consequences of these limitations and their implications for knowledge production.
